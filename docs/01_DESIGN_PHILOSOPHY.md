# 01 — Design Philosophy

## The Central Idea: The Explorer's Atlas

This portfolio is not a resume rendered as a website.

It is an **atlas of explored territories** — a living record of an engineer who crosses boundaries between systems, languages, platforms, and ideas, and documents what he finds.

Every design decision flows from this single concept. The metaphor is not decorative. It is structural. It determines what goes on every page, how sections relate to each other, what the loader communicates, why the color system was chosen, and what a visitor feels when they leave.

---

## Identity: The Explorer

Heramb is not a specialist who goes deep in one domain. He is not a generalist who skims the surface of everything. He is an **explorer** — someone who moves deliberately across territories, establishes real competency in each, finds connections that specialists miss, and brings those connections back as built things.

This is a quality of mind, not a list of technologies.

The portfolio must communicate this quality **before the visitor reads a single word**. The visual language, the loading experience, the way sections reveal themselves, the structure of a case study — all of it must say: *this person thinks in systems and moves across them with intention.*

---

## The Atlas Concept

Every element of the portfolio maps to the Atlas metaphor:

| Element | Atlas Metaphor |
|---|---|
| Loader | A map being drawn before the world appears |
| Hero section | The central territory — where the explorer stands today |
| Projects | Each one is its own world — different terrain, different visual language |
| Skills section | The equipment carried through expeditions |
| Notes | The expedition diary — raw thinking made beautiful |
| Timeline | The route taken |
| Knowledge graph | How all territories connect |
| Case studies | Field reports from completed expeditions |

This is not window dressing. When building any new section, ask: *what does this mean in the atlas?* If the answer is unclear, the section needs rethinking.

---

## Storytelling Philosophy

The portfolio tells a story without stating it.

It does **not** say: "I am an explorer who works across many domains." Instead, it demonstrates this claim through structure:

- The project cards each have their own visual identity (color, tone, treatment) — proving that each project was its own world, not a copy-paste exercise
- The notes connect to projects — proving that the builder thinks in public and documents decisions
- The skills Bento is sized by usage frequency — proving self-awareness about competency, not just listing technologies
- The timeline shows a real trajectory — proving growth, not just a snapshot

The visitor should feel they understand who built this **before** they consciously process why.

## Engineering Craftsmanship

The portfolio should feel like it was built by someone who **cares about the details no one asked for**.

This is not about visual complexity. Bruno Simon's portfolio (the reference) is technically overwhelming but emotionally simple: *this person cared so much they built an entire world.* The craft is the message.

In this portfolio, craftsmanship manifests as:

- Transitions that have a reason to exist
- Hover states that communicate something about the element's nature
- Typography that creates genuine hierarchy, not just size differences
- Code that is organized the way a senior engineer would write it, even though no recruiter will ever read it
- Empty states that are designed, not broken

The standard is: **if this detail were removed, would something be lost?** If yes, the detail stays. If no, it gets cut.

---

## Emotional Design Goals

When someone closes this portfolio tab, they should carry one impression: **this was built by an engineer who cares.**

Not impressed by technology. Not impressed by visual effects. Not informed by credentials.

They should carry a *feeling* — the same way you hum a song without remembering the lyrics.

The emotional arc of the visit:

1. **Arrival (Loader)**: Something is being built. There is precision here.
2. **Hero**: I understand who this person is and what they value in under 10 seconds.
3. **Projects**: Each of these is its own world. This person didn't just follow tutorials.
4. **Skills**: Honest, self-aware, connected to real work.
5. **Notes**: This person thinks in public. They process what they build.
6. **Case Studies**: I understand how this person solves problems, not just what they built.
7. **Departure**: I want to talk to this person.

## The Three Audiences

### For Recruiters (12 seconds)
They need: name, what you do, proof you can do it, how to reach you.
The portfolio must communicate all four in the first viewport.
The Hero carries this responsibility entirely.

### For Engineers (8 minutes)
They want: how do you think? what tradeoffs do you make? do you understand your own work?
The Case Studies and Notes carry this responsibility.
An engineer who reads one case study should feel they could have a technical conversation with you.

### For Startup Founders (3 minutes)
They want: can this person move fast? do they build real things? are they curious?
The breadth of the project portfolio and the notes system carry this.
A founder should see evidence of shipping, learning, iterating.

---

## Why This Portfolio Must Feel Handcrafted

Most developer portfolios fail because they feel assembled, not built.

They use templates, frameworks-within-frameworks, default shadcn themes, standard Tailwind configurations. The result communicates exactly the opposite of what a portfolio should: it says *I ship things quickly by using defaults* rather than *I understand every decision I make.*

This portfolio must feel like no defaults were accepted without interrogation. Every color must have a reason. Every animation must have a purpose. Every section must earn its place.

The benchmark is simple: if someone looked at this portfolio and could identify the template it was built on, we have failed.
